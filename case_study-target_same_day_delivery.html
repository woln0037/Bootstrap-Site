Setting the Scene

Imagine being a shopper for Shipt. You receive a customer’s order from Target’s platform, spend time carefully shopping for each item the guest needs, communicate with them if anything is out of stock, check out, and deliver the order straight to their home only to receive a poor tip and little feedback on how to improve..

This would be deflating for any shopper, and Shipt has started to notice that tips and ratings are lower on Target’s Platform orders as compared to Shipt’s Marketplace orders. Why might this be and how can we improve this?

The problem extends beyond just poor tips and lack of feedback. Like a chain of dominoes, one thing leads to another:

  1. Shoppers aren’t getting tipped well for their efforts, and in many cases aren’t receiving feedback on how to improve either. This results in Shoppers picking up less Target Platform orders.
  2. Target has a hard time filling orders because shoppers aren’t as willing to shop and deliver Target orders.
  3. Target guests aren’t getting their orders efficiently and wait times are increasing.
  4. Shipt gets pressure from Target and they have to start paying out incentives to convince Shipt shoppers to shop and deliver Target orders.

Q: What is Shipt?

A: From Shipt's website: “Shipt is a membership-based grocery marketplace, enabling delivery of fresh foods, household essentials, and more from local stores.”

Q: What is Target’s Same Day Delivery service?

A: From Target's website: “Same Day Delivery (shopped by Shipt) from Target.com and the Target app provide delivery from your local Target store via a local community of reliable shoppers.”

The Beginning

Picking up the ideas from the lead designer, I dove into research and discovery to validate which of the ideas I should prioritize. My first step was to audit the current Target Platform (Same Day Delivery) flow next to the current Shipt Marketplace flow. At first glance, the flows are pretty much the same, but paying close attention to the details yields some eye-opening insights.

No major differences to note here.

Shipt’s prompt here was more shopper-focused, had more personality, and was more descriptive.

Target took a rating-first approach, where Shipt put the tipping experience first.

Target enables the full experience after the star rating value is selected, Shipt has two separate screens.

The key takeaways are:

  1. Target could be more shopper-focused in our language and consider embedding more personality and descriptiveness.
  2. Target could consider putting the tip experience first, and/or separating out the screens.

The Top Priority:

Shipt elevated concerns from shoppers that they have been receiving feedback about overall experience concerns out of their control. In order to make sure shoppers are getting productive feedback, it is important to make the shopper the center of the prompt for feedback. My hypothesis is that once we align Target's app and web experiences to focus on soliciting shopper-specific feedback, that shoppers will get what they need to improve their service and hopefully their tips.

The good news is, when I brought my findings to the team, the Product Owner wrote a ticket for Engineering to implement this change immediately.

Shifting Focus to Increase Tips

With a win under my belt re-focusing feedback on the shopper, I shifted my focus to how I can alter the current UI and flow to encourage guest to give their shoppers better tips based on their performance.

My hypothesis is that if we can encourage guests to tip based on their shopper rating, tips will increase. There is currently a high rate of guests tipping poorly if at all even on 5-star orders and this needs to change.

Taking what I learned from critique, I made some tweaks then set up a meeting with our Design Systems team to get their input on what was possible. We worked together and found that keeping things simple by using existing components was the best option. The final design will recommend a tip for the guest to give their shopper based on how they rate the shopper.


  • If the guest gives the shopper 5-stars, we recommend the guest tips their shopper 20% for stellar service.
  • A rating of 3 or 4-stars will recommend the guest tips their shopper 15%.
  • In the event that the guest rates their shopper 1 or 2-stars, we will still recommend a tip of 10% for the shoppers efforts.

We considered removing the "No Tip" line right away, but wanted to test the above concept without changing too many variables.

A Jira story was written and accepted and the A/B test went live. It was time to await the results to see if my hypothesis was correct.

A/B Testing Results

The results were quite exciting! There was a clear increase in tips where we placed a "Recommended" label. The graphs speak for themselves.

As you can see in the following 5 graphs, the purple line on the graph showing our treatment of adding a "Recommended" label yielded an increase in 20% tips in general compared to the control (represented by the light blue line). The pattern held true for 3 & 4-Star 15% tips, as well as 1 & 2-Star 10% tips. The margin between our treatment and the control decreased progressively as star rating decreased likely because it is more rare for shoppers to receive low star ratings.

Results for recommending a 20% tip for 5-Star Reviews:

Guests tipped an average of about 4% more when we tested the "Recommended" label treatment against the control group. About 10k more guests tipped 20% when there was a "Recommended" label placed next to the 20% tip option. I was hoping that the number of guests opting for "No Tip" would convert to tipping 20% instead, but this wasn't the case.


Results for recommending a 15% tip for 4-Star Reviews:

Guests tipped an average of about 12% more when we tested the "Recommended" label treatment against the control group. About 1.7k more guests tipped 15% when there was a "Recommended" label placed next to the 15% tip option. In this tipping tier, we did see a decrease in the number of guests opting for "No Tip" but not as large of a decrease as we'd hoped.


Results for recommending a 15% tip for 3-Star Reviews:

Guests tipped an average of about 5% more when we tested the "Recommended" label treatment against the control group, but there wasn't a significant number of guests changing their tipping behavior at this tip tier and the control and treatment results are very similar.


Results for recommending a 10% tip for 2-Star Reviews:

Guests tipped an average of about 35% more when we tested the "Recommended" label treatment against the control group, but there wasn't a significant number of guests changing their tipping behavior at this tip tier and the control and treatment results are very similar. One observation I did make at this tip tier though, was that the "No Tip" number decreased.


Results for recommending a 10% tip for 1-Star Reviews:

Guests tipped an average of about 41% more when we tested the "Recommended" label treatment against the control group, but there wasn't a significant number of guests changing their tipping behavior at this tip tier. Less guests selected "No Tip" but not a very significant number.


Next Steps

With the success of the A/B test, the change went live. We wanted to push things one step further and investigate what might happen if we removed the "No Tip" option all-together. "No Tip" is currently the most-selected option as shown in the following bar graph:

With a "Custom Amount" option, we felt comfortable knowing the guest could still enter $0 if their decision still was not to tip. We are hoping this change will deter the large volume of "No Tip" guests and help them to realize that tipping is important to compensate shoppers for their efforts.
