Development & Design

Define the Problem Statement:

During user interviews and surveying, I discovered that it's really important for people to maximize & optimize the time they spend on their trips.

Therefore, I believe that there is unnecessary stress around planning the key elements of a trip. How might I help my user whittle down a high volume of options and identify what's most important to them so they can make the best use of time on their trip?

Define Key Priorities/Features:

Using the interview data, and what I know about my target user, I created 14 of each of the following: “I like”, “I wish”, “What if” to begin whittling down the key features for my travel app. After sorting out the data, some key categories emerged:

In order to prioritize my key features, I used a few tools.

First, I created a “How, Now, Wow” graph as a way to gather my assumptions.

Next, I took my final 14 “I wish” and “What if” statements and created a survey to see how each statement resonated with the participants. They had 4 options to choose from for each statement:

A: I would use the feature AND be interested in it
B: I would use the feature BUT not be interested in it
C: I would NOT use the feature BUT be interested in it
D: I would NOT use the feature AND not be interested in it

I received 24 responses and was able to create graphs to help me create a “MoSCoW” graph.

I then was able to layer the “How, Now, Wow” (Figure 1) and “MoSCoW” (Figure 2) graphs to whittle down the features. I grayed out the areas of low interest/priority, and was able to box-in the highest priorities based on the data received which is what you see below in Figures 1 & 2.

Ravel’s Value Proposition:

Ravel is an app designed to help post-pandemic travelers save time when planning travel by:

  1. providing ideas
  2. optimizing routes
  3. referencing data from past searches and trips.

Ravel stands out because it offers: sophisticated advanced filtering, customized user recommendations, and travel plan history & documentation all in one thoughtful place. We’re believable because all of our features double-down on saving our user time and providing peace of mind in every step of the process.

Guerrilla User Testing Prep:

Taking my digital wireframes and clickable prototype, I conducted user tests with 4 users. I wanted to keep my tasks broad and slightly vague to see how each user would interact. The following were the 5 tasks I assigned my users:


  1. Sign up for a Ravel account with Facebook & opt to complete your profile later
  2. Sign out of Ravel and reset your password
  3. Enable the “Get ideas” feature on the app
  4. Search for something and narrow down your options by filtering, then add it to your itinerary
  5. Optimize your route with the map feature

I wrote down the steps I thought they would take before performing the tests with them, and observed what they did.

Final Thoughts:



Lean into user research and don’t let personal assumptions seep in: I found myself wanting to dive right in to designing because I assumed I knew which features people would gravitate towards. This was my first experience performing a case study and designing an app prototype- sometimes I got caught up in getting the deliverable done and lost sight of the user I had identified and how all parts of the process link together.

Get users to interact with the prototype as soon as possible: I wish I would have had users interact with my early InVision prototype instead of waiting until the digital wireframes. I think it could have saved me a lot of rework to get early prototypes in front of users. I’d like to learn how to present rough wireframes to users better- many of my users got hung up on “placeholders” and wanted to be able to truly interact with the prototype.

Sharing work is important: I found myself getting really protective of my homework deliverables because of the large amount of time they took me to complete. I wish my peers and I would have shared our work more so we could have helped each other learn more and improve our own concepts. The few times people asked me to share my work, I got really worried that they may copy my work, but in hindsight as long as I’m learning and staying true to myself, sharing work is a good thing!
